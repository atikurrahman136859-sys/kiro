A complete operating model for an online-only 360° digital services provider that helps clients launch, build, market, digitize, optimize, and scale across the full digital landscape (web, e-commerce, search, social, content, paid media, email, automation, and data). Every capability here is delivered digitally; we do not provide offline or physical-media services (no out-of-home, print, retail, or field activation).

## Part 1 — The Core Idea

Most agencies sell services. We sell outcomes across a journey. Every client moves through 7 lifecycle phases. Every one of our service lines has a role to play in each phase. The intersection of "phase" and "service line" is where revenue, integration, and long-term retention are created. A service is never "done" — it evolves with the client from phase to phase.

### The operating philosophy (5 principles)

- One client, one journey, one account team. The client experiences a single relationship, not a collection of disconnected projects.
- Every service has a "next service." Each deliverable is designed to create the natural need for the following one (research, brand, website, ads, retention, scaling).
- Every digital channel is one connected strategy, not separate silos. Search, social, content, paid media, email, and on-site experience are orchestrated as a single funnel with one set of numbers.
- Data is the connective tissue. A shared analytics layer lets every team see the same truth and prove ROI continuously.
- Retainers over transactions. The goal of every project is to earn the right to the next phase and convert the client into a recurring growth partnership.

## Part 2 — Complete Client Lifecycle Map

The client lifecycle is the backbone of the entire 360° model. Every client — whether a pre-launch startup or an established enterprise — can be located on this map, and our job is to move them forward, phase by phase, without ever losing the relationship.

The journey flows: Phase 1 Discovery, then Phase 2 Foundation & Brand, then Phase 3 Digital Presence, then Phase 4 Go-To-Market & Launch, then Phase 5 Operations, CX & Retention, then Phase 6 Scaling & Expansion, then Phase 7 Transformation & Innovation. The lifecycle is a flywheel, not a finish line: a healthy client re-enters earlier phases for new products, markets, and reinventions (for example, Phase 7 loops back to Phase 1, and Phase 5 loops back to Phase 3 for repeat launches).

For every phase we define six things, so the whole company shares one mental model: client objective, client challenges, key activities, services connected, deliverables, and the exit trigger that signals readiness for the next phase (the cross-sell moment).

### The lifecycle as a maturity model

| Maturity score | Meaning | Our motion |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 0 | Not started | Sell the entry project for that phase |
| 1 to 2 | Started, weak | Audit and rebuild |
| 3 | Functional | Optimize and integrate |
| 4 | Strong | Scale and automate |
| 5 | Best-in-class | Innovate and maintain on retainer |

Account-team rule: never present a single service in isolation. Always show the client where they are on the map and what the next two phases unlock. This is what turns a project buyer into a journey buyer.

### Cross-phase service journeys

Each service line has a story that runs the full length of the lifecycle. Selling the journey (not the task) is how a one-off becomes a multi-year retainer.

- Branding: Research, then Identity, then Website visuals, then Campaign creative, then CX & reputation, then Sub-brands, then Rebrand. The positioning from Phase 1 informs the identity in Phase 2, which drives the website in Phase 3 and the ads in Phase 4, and is protected and evolved in Phases 5 to 7.
- Digital Marketing: SEO/market gap, then Channel plan, then SEO setup, then Launch ads, then Retargeting/retention, then Scaling campaigns, then Predictive media. Audience data captured at launch fuels retargeting, which trains the predictive models that make scaling efficient.
- Technology: CRM, then Website, then Automation, then Analytics, then ERP/support, then Scalable infra, then AI platforms. The CRM is the spine; every later system plugs into it, deepening the partnership.
- Operations: SOPs, then Workflows, then Team systems, then Automation, then Multi-site/franchise, then Transformation.
- Sales & Revenue: Revenue model, then CRM, then Funnel, then Pipeline/CRO, then Renewals & upsell, then Channel/partner, then Revenue intelligence.

### The non-negotiable integration handoffs

Phase integration is vertical. Service-to-service integration is horizontal, and it is where most agencies fail. These are the critical handoffs to engineer.

| From, to | What flows | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Strategy to Branding | Positioning, personas, naming | Brand is built on validated insight, not taste |
| Branding to Web/Tech | Identity system, assets, UX direction | Website expresses the brand consistently |
| Web/Tech to Marketing | Pixels, events, landing pages, attribution | Ads send traffic to optimized, tracked destinations |
| Marketing to Sales/CRM | Qualified leads plus source data | No leads lost; sales knows context |
| Sales/CRM to CX | Customer records, history | Onboarding and support are personalized |
| CX to Data | Feedback, NPS, churn signals | Retention is measured and acted on |
| Everything to Data to Strategy | KPIs, ROI, cohorts | Strategy is re-informed every quarter (the flywheel) |
| Finance and Strategy | Budgets, unit economics, LTV/CAC | Decisions are grounded in money, not vibes |

The single source of truth is the CRM plus analytics layer — the shared nervous system. Every service writes to it and reads from it. This is what makes the 360° model integrated rather than merely bundled.

### Integration anti-patterns to avoid

| Anti-pattern | Symptom | Fix |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Siloed delivery | Each team emails files and disappears | Shared account team plus single project space |
| Re-discovery tax | Every team re-asks the client the same questions | One client brief plus shared CRM record |
| Untracked launches | Ads run with no pixels or attribution | Web/Tech sets up tracking before Marketing spends |
| Orphaned leads | Marketing generates leads no one works | Mandatory Marketing-to-Sales SLA and routing |
| Vanity reporting | Each team reports its own metric | One KPI tree rolling up to client outcomes |

## Part 4 — The Business Ecosystem

The whole company is a connected ecosystem: the client at the center, service lines orbiting them, a shared data core binding everything, and the lifecycle flywheel driving recurring growth.

### Ecosystem overview — client at the center

The client is the hub. Nine service lines orbit and serve them: Strategy & Consulting, Branding & Creative, Web & Technology, Marketing & Growth, Sales & CRM, Customer Experience, Operations, Finance & Legal, and Innovation & AI. A shared data plus CRM core sits beneath the relationship; every service both feeds it and draws from it. That core is what makes us integrated, not just full-service.

### The growth flywheel

Each turn makes the next turn easier and cheaper, because data, brand equity, and trust compound: Insight (research + data), then Brand & Presence (identity + digital), then Demand (marketing + sales), then Experience (CX + operations), then Retention & LTV (loyalty + analytics), then Scale & Expansion (growth + finance), then Reinvestment (innovation + new ventures), and back to Insight. Each stage produces an asset the next stage consumes. Momentum is the moat.

### The three layers of the ecosystem

| Layer | Purpose | Owner |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 — Client Experience (front stage) | The single, seamless relationship the client feels: one account team, one 360° roadmap, one dashboard | Account / Growth Partner Lead |
| 2 — Service Delivery (back stage) | The nine specialist service lines that do the work | Service-line directors |
| 3 — Shared Infrastructure (foundation) | The CRM and data warehouse, project/workflow system, knowledge base/SOP library, and automation/AI layer | Operations / RevOps |

### How the ecosystem creates healthy lock-in

More connected services lead to more shared data, which leads to better and faster results, which leads to more trust, which leads to more connected services — and better results also drive higher client LTV. Lock-in here is earned, not contractual: the more of the ecosystem a client uses, the richer their shared data, the better our results, the deeper the trust. This loop is the commercial heart of the model.

## Part 5 — Revenue Opportunities Per Phase

The 360° model is a revenue system, not a price list. Each phase opens specific revenue streams, and each project should convert into recurring revenue and into the next phase's project. The dollar signs below indicate relative value (more signs = higher value); calibrate to your market, currency, and cost base before quoting.

### The three revenue types

| Type | Description | Margin profile | Strategic role |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Project (one-off) | Fixed-scope deliverable (website, brand) | Medium to high | Entry point; proof of value |
| Retainer (recurring) | Ongoing monthly service (marketing, support, ops) | High and predictable | The core of enterprise value |
| Performance / equity | Revenue share, rev-per-result, or equity | Variable, high upside | Aligns incentives; premium positioning |

Target portfolio mix at maturity: about 60% retainer, 30% project, 10% performance/equity. Retainers create the predictable base; projects create entry and expansion; performance deals create upside and differentiation.

### Client lifetime value

A client who enters at Phase 1 and is nurtured through the lifecycle is worth multiples of a one-off buyer, because value accumulates: a strategy project leads to a brand project, which leads to a website project plus a hosting retainer, which leads to a launch project plus a performance-marketing retainer, which leads to CX/analytics/automation retainers, which lead to expansion projects plus performance share, which lead to transformation programs plus an intelligence retainer. Cumulative LTV grows with every phase.

LTV levers we actively manage: cross-sell rate (average number of service lines per client), retainer attach rate (percent of projects that convert to recurring revenue), phase progression rate (percent of clients who advance), and net revenue retention (recurring revenue growth within existing accounts).

### The cross-sell and upsell engine

The engine runs: Entry project (any phase), then Prove value (a clear KPI win), then Convert to retainer, then Expand to an adjacent service, then Advance to the next phase, and back to proving value. Rules of the engine: land with a clear, fast win; always quote the retainer alongside the project; review quarterly using the maturity model to surface the next phase; bundle adjacent services at a better rate; and tie performance fees to metrics on the shared dashboard so upside is transparent.

### Packaging tiers

| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| A la carte projects | Single deliverables | Project | New or cautious clients |
| Phase package | All services for one phase, bundled | Project plus entry retainer | Clients at a clear inflection point |

Positioning line for sales: "You can buy a project, or you can buy a growth partner. The project gets you one result; the partnership gets you every result, connected — and it costs less per outcome because everything compounds."

## Part 6 — Digital Channel & Funnel Strategy

As an online-only 360° digital provider, our edge is not "we run ads." It is that we orchestrate every digital channel as one customer, one journey, one funnel, one measurement model — instead of letting SEO, paid, social, email, and on-site experience run as disconnected silos with their own budgets and their own vanity reports.

### The guiding principle: one funnel, every channel

The customer does not think in channels. They discover a brand on social, search it, read reviews, get retargeted, buy on the site, and get nurtured by email. The journey loops: Awareness (SEO/content, paid social, paid search, video, organic social, digital PR/influencer), then Consideration (website/app, retargeting, reviews/reputation, email capture), then Conversion (e-commerce checkout, landing pages/CRO, marketplaces), then Retention & Advocacy (email/SMS, loyalty/referrals, support, online community), and back to Awareness. Every touchpoint writes into the same customer profile — stitched by pixels, UTMs, and CRM IDs — so the journey is continuous, attributable, and optimizable.

### The connection mechanisms

A "360° digital" promise is only real if the channels are technically wired together. These mechanisms keep the funnel unbroken and attributable. Rule: no channel goes live without tracking and CRM wiring in place.

| Mechanism | Connects | What it enables |
| --- | --- | --- |
| UTM tagging (consistent taxonomy) | Every campaign to analytics | Clean source/medium attribution |
| Pixels & server-side tracking | Ads and site events | Accurate conversion data, better optimization |
| CRM / customer ID stitching | Anonymous to known user | One profile across sessions and channels |
| Shared audiences | CRM and ad platforms | Retargeting, lookalikes, suppression lists |

### Budget allocation logic

Allocate by job-to-be-done and measured contribution, not by the channel each specialist likes to defend: start with an awareness/conversion/retention split appropriate to the phase (early phases lean awareness; later phases lean conversion and retention); measure every channel's contribution to the unified funnel (including assisted conversions, not just last-click); reallocate monthly toward the channels with the best marginal return; and protect a fixed percent for top-of-funnel/brand so the funnel keeps filling. The 360° digital advantage is that because we run every channel and measure them in one model, we can move budget to whatever works.

## Part 7 — Client Retention Model

Retention is the whole point. A 360° firm's enterprise value lives in recurring revenue and multi-year relationships. This model covers both retaining our clients and helping clients retain their customers. Both run on the same logic: deliver value, prove it, deepen the relationship, repeat.

### Retaining our clients — the retention ladder

The ladder climbs: Project buyer (one service), then Repeat buyer (more projects), then Retainer client (recurring), then Multi-service partner (several lines), then Growth partner (strategic, multi-year), then Advocate (referrals plus case study). Each rung increases retention, margin, and lifetime value. The account team's job is to move every client up the ladder.

### What drives agency retention

| Driver | How we operationalize it |
| --- | --- |
| Proven ROI | One shared dashboard that ties our work to the client's revenue |
| Single relationship | One account/growth-partner lead across all services |
| Proactive roadmap | Quarterly business review that always proposes the next phase |
| Switching cost (earned) | Integrated data and systems make us part of their operating fabric |
| Responsiveness | SLAs, fast communication, no dropped balls |
| Relationship depth | Multiple stakeholders on both sides, not a single point of contact |

### The Quarterly Business Review (QBR) — the retention engine

Every retainer client gets a QBR structured as: Results (what we did and what it produced, KPI deltas versus targets), then Insights (what the data is telling us — the part they cannot get elsewhere), then Maturity check (re-score them on the lifecycle map), then Roadmap (the next one to two phases and the projects that unlock them), then Proposal (a concrete, costed next step). No QBR should end without a confirmed continuation and a proposed expansion.

### Churn early-warning system

| Signal | Risk | Play |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Falling engagement (slow replies, skipped QBRs) | High | Executive check-in, re-establish value |
| Flat or negative KPIs | High | Diagnostic plus reset plan before they ask |
| Single point of contact leaves | High | Multi-thread the relationship fast |
| Scope shrinking | Medium | Re-discover needs; re-map maturity |
| Price sensitivity / discount requests | Medium | Re-anchor on ROI and outcomes |
| Going quiet near renewal | High | Proactive renewal conversation 60+ days out |

### Helping clients retain their customers (the Phase 5 service)

This is what we sell, and it mirrors our own model. The customer value retention loop runs: Acquire, then Onboard (first value fast), then Engage (lifecycle comms), then Reward (loyalty), then Advocate (referrals/reviews), and back to Acquire — with a Recover (win-back) path whenever engagement signals risk.

| Lifecycle marketing | Segmented email/SMS, triggered campaigns | Marketing + Automation |

The mirror principle: we sell retention because we practice retention. The same loop — deliver value, prove it, deepen the relationship, expand — runs at both levels. Retention is both our service and our business model.

## Part 8 — Internal Team Structure by Phase

A 360° firm needs an org design that delivers a single relationship to the client while keeping deep specialist capability inside. The answer is a pod plus practice matrix: client-facing pods own the relationship; specialist practices own the craft.

### The operating model: pods x practices

- Pods are cross-functional, client-facing teams led by a Growth Partner Lead. They own the client roadmap, retention, and results across all phases.
- Practices are the centers of excellence (Strategy; Brand & Creative; Web & Tech; Marketing; Sales/CRM; CX & Ops; Data & AI; Finance & Legal). Specialists live in a practice for craft, standards, and career growth, and are deployed into pods.
- Shared services (RevOps/PMO, Talent/L&D, Finance/Admin) keep the firm running.

This is how firms stay integrated and deep: the client feels one team (the pod), while quality is guaranteed by the practices behind it.

## Part 9 — SOP & Workflow Recommendations

SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures) turn a 360° idea into a 360° machine. They make delivery consistent, integration reliable, and the firm scalable, because quality stops depending on heroics and starts depending on systems.

### The SOP stack

| Tier | Examples | Owner |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 — Firm-wide | Onboarding, QBR, billing, escalation, data governance | Operations / PMO |
| 2 — Lifecycle | Discovery process, launch process, retention process | Pod / Practice leads |
| 3 — Service | "How we build a website", "How we run a paid campaign" | Practice leads |
| 4 — Tool/Task | "Set up GA4", "Configure CRM pipeline", "Build a retargeting audience" | Specialists |

### The master client workflow

The end-to-end flow: Lead/inbound, then Qualify & maturity scan, then Proposal & roadmap, then Onboarding (intake once), then Kickoff & baseline KPIs, then Deliver (pod plus practices), then Review & report, then QBR & next-phase proposal — which loops back to Deliver, or moves to Renew/Expand. The loop from QBR to Deliver is the recurring-revenue engine; the workflow is deliberately circular, not linear.

### Foundational SOPs (build these first)

- SOP 1 — Client Onboarding (the most important): create the client record in the shared CRM; run intake once (goals, brand, access, stakeholders, history) and store centrally; set KPI baselines and targets; assign the pod and introduce the Growth Partner Lead; grant access to the shared dashboard and channel; schedule the kickoff and the first QBR. Outcome: no team ever re-asks the client a question we already have an answer to.
- SOP 2 — Discovery & Proposal: run the maturity scan to locate the client on the lifecycle map; identify the lead phase plus the next one to two phases; produce a roadmap-first proposal (not a service list); always present a project plus retainer option.
- SOP 3 — Service Handoff (the integration SOP): upstream team completes the handoff checklist (assets, data, access); downstream team confirms receipt before starting (no silent starts); handoff logged with owner and date. This single SOP eliminates the number-one failure of integrated agencies: dropped handoffs.
- SOP 4 — Reporting & QBR: all teams report into one KPI tree, not separate reports; monthly snapshot auto-generated from the analytics layer; quarterly QBR moves from results to insights to maturity to roadmap to proposal.
- SOP 5 — Escalation & Issue Management: severity levels defined (P1 outage to P4 minor); response-time SLAs per level; clear escalation path from Specialist to Delivery Manager to Growth Partner Lead to Director.

### Service-line workflow templates (with gates)

- Website build: Brief, Architecture/sitemap, Design, Build, Content, QA, Tracking setup, Launch, Handoff to Marketing. Gate: tracking and analytics must be live before Marketing drives traffic.
- Paid campaign: Objective, Audience, Creative, Tracking check, Launch, Daily monitor, Weekly optimize, Monthly report. Gate: pixels/UTMs verified and lead routing to CRM confirmed before spend.
- Brand identity: Strategy input, Moodboard, Concepts, Refinement, Guidelines, Asset kit, Handoff to Web/Marketing. Gate: positioning from Strategy received before design starts.
- Retention program: Baseline metrics, Segment, Design journeys, Build automation, Launch, Measure, Optimize. Gate: CRM data clean and segmented before journey build.

### Workflow principles and automation

Principles: single source of truth (one CRM plus analytics layer everyone reads/writes); capture once, reuse everywhere; gates before spend/build; confirmed handoffs; automate the repetitive; document as you go (every recurring task becomes a Tier-4 SOP); templatize deliverables. High-value automation opportunities include lead intake and routing, onboarding tasks, reporting, follow-ups and nurture, QBR prep, and internal handoffs. Treat the SOP/knowledge library as intellectual property and enterprise value: it makes the firm scalable, sellable, and consistent. Review SOPs quarterly; every recurring mistake becomes a new or updated SOP.

## Part 10 — KPI Tracking System

The KPI system is the shared truth that holds the 360° model together. It proves ROI to the client (drives retention), lets every service line see the same numbers (drives integration), and lets leadership run the firm (drives the business). One KPI tree, not one report per team: every team's metric must roll up to a client outcome and ultimately to revenue.

### The KPI pyramid

From top to bottom: North-star (client revenue growth plus retention), then Outcome KPIs (leads, sales, LTV, churn, ROI), then Channel KPIs (SEO, paid, CX, ops, sales), then Activity KPIs (tasks, outputs, delivery health). Activity is what we do (leading, controllable); Channel is how each discipline performs; Outcome is what the client gets (the proof); North-star is the client's growth plus the relationship's health. When a north-star number moves, you can trace down the pyramid to the cause.

### KPIs by lifecycle phase

| Phase | Primary KPIs | Leading indicators |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 Discovery | Strategy delivered, decisions enabled | Research completeness, insight quality, client confidence |
| 2 Foundation | Brand plus legal plus ops live | Time-to-setup, asset completeness, compliance done |
| 3 Digital | Site live, conversion-ready | Page speed, mobile UX score, tracking coverage, funnel completeness |
| 4 Launch | CAC, leads, first sales, ROAS | CTR, CPC, conversion rate, lead volume, lead quality |
| 5 Operate/Retain | Retention rate, CSAT/NPS, LTV, efficiency | Repeat rate, response time, ticket resolution, automation coverage |
| 6 Scale | Revenue growth, new-market traction, LTV:CAC | Scaled ROAS, market penetration, ops capacity |
| 7 Transform | Innovation adoption, future-readiness | AI/automation usage, new-revenue percent, transformation milestones |

### Dashboards and cadence

Three dashboards read from one data layer/warehouse fed by web/analytics, ad platforms, CRM/sales, support/CX, finance, and marketplace/affiliate sources: the Client dashboard (outcomes, ROI, funnel, retention — the proof), the Internal Ops dashboard (delivery health, KPIs by channel, utilization), and the Exec dashboard (MRR/ARR, NRR, margin, pipeline). Every channel flows into the same warehouse so all digital channels live in one funnel. Reporting cadence: real-time/daily (spend, pacing, anomalies, outages for delivery teams); weekly (channel performance and optimizations for pod plus client lead); monthly (outcome KPIs versus targets for client plus pod); quarterly QBR (results, insights, maturity, roadmap for client execs); quarterly internal (firm KPIs, margins, retention for leadership).

Principles for KPIs that work: tie every metric to revenue/retention; baseline first; set targets and owners, not just actuals; one source of truth; track leading plus lagging; make it visible; and always act on it (each review ends in decisions).
